Breast Cancer Genetic Testing: What You Should Know About BRCA Genes

Premier Hematology & Oncology
2025 · 5 min read

Genetic testing has revolutionized the field of oncology, enabling individuals to understand their hereditary risk for certain types of cancer—most notably breast and ovarian cancer.

Among the most well-known forms of cancer genetic testing is BRCA testing, which identifies mutations in the BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes.

These genes play a critical role in DNA repair and maintaining cell growth regulation.

When they are mutated, the likelihood of developing certain cancers increases significantly.

This comprehensive guide aims to help you understand what BRCA genetic testing is, who should consider it, and how to interpret the results.

It also discusses how this cancer genetic testing can influence medical decision-making for both patients and their families.

What Are BRCA Genes?

BRCA1 and BRCA2—short for BReast CAncer gene 1 and BReast CAncer gene 2—are tumor suppressor genes.

They produce proteins essential for repairing damaged DNA, thereby ensuring the stability of a cell’s genetic material.

When either of these genes is mutated, DNA damage may not be repaired properly, leading to increased cancer risk.

Individuals with BRCA mutations are more likely to develop breast, ovarian, and other types of cancers.

What is BRCA Testing?

BRCA testing is a form of cancer genetic testing that analyzes an individual’s DNA to detect mutations in the BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes.

The process typically involves a blood draw or saliva sample.

A laboratory then examines the DNA for pathogenic (harmful) variants.

BRCA testing helps assess a person’s risk of developing certain cancers.

If you carry a BRCA mutation, your risk of breast cancer by the age of 80 may be up to six times higher than that of the general population.

Similarly, the risk of ovarian cancer, prostate cancer, and pancreatic cancer may also increase.

Who Should Consider BRCA Testing?

While BRCA mutations are rare—affecting approximately 0.2% of the U.S. population—they are more common in certain groups.

Cancer genetic testing for BRCA mutations is generally recommended for individuals with one or more of the following risk factors:

  • Personal history of breast cancer, especially if diagnosed before age 50
  • Family member with a known BRCA mutation
  • Family history of male breast cancer
  • Diagnosis of both breast and ovarian cancer
  • Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry
  • Family history of multiple types of cancer
  • Known hereditary cancer syndromes (e.g., Li-Fraumeni, Cowden syndrome, Peutz-Jeghers syndrome, Fanconi anemia)

If any of these apply, your healthcare provider may suggest genetic counseling and BRCA testing.

The BRCA Testing Process

Before the Test

Before undergoing cancer genetic testing, you’ll typically consult a genetic counselor.

This specialist will evaluate your personal and family health history and discuss the implications of testing. Topics covered include:

  • The meaning of a positive, negative, or inconclusive result
  • The impact of results on your treatment and prevention strategies
  • How your results might affect family members

During the Test

BRCA testing is straightforward. A healthcare provider will draw a small vial of blood or collect a saliva sample. The entire process takes only a few minutes and carries minimal risk.

After the Test

Results are usually available within a few weeks.

Once the results are in, another appointment with your genetic counselor will help you understand what they mean and determine your next steps.

Understanding BRCA Test Results

Positive Result

A positive BRCA test means that you carry a harmful mutation in either the BRCA1 or BRCA2 gene. This increases your lifetime risk for various cancers, including:

  • Breast cancer
  • Ovarian cancer and related cancers like fallopian tube and peritoneal cancer
  • Prostate cancer
  • Pancreatic cancer
  • Fanconi anemia (in rare cases, particularly for BRCA2 mutations)

While a positive result doesn’t guarantee cancer development, it significantly raises your risk.

With this information, you and your doctor can consider proactive measures, such as:

  • Increased surveillance (e.g., annual mammograms, breast MRIs)
  • Chemoprevention using drugs like tamoxifen
  • Preventive surgeries (e.g., mastectomy or removal of ovaries and fallopian tubes)
  • Lifestyle changes and early intervention strategies

Negative Result

A negative result indicates no detectable BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutations.

This suggests your risk is similar to the general population.

However, it does not eliminate the possibility of cancer—other gene mutations or environmental factors may still influence risk.

If you have a strong family history of cancer despite a negative BRCA test, your provider may recommend more extensive cancer genetic testing using a broader gene panel.

Variant of Unknown Significance (VUS)

Sometimes the test identifies a gene variant whose impact is not well understood.

This is known as a Variant of Unknown Significance.

Your genetic counselor will help interpret these ambiguous results and may recommend additional testing or close monitoring.

Goals of Cancer Genetic Testing in Breast Cancer

One primary goal of BRCA testing is to guide surgical and treatment decisions. For instance:

  • Women who are BRCA-positive may opt for prophylactic mastectomy to significantly reduce breast cancer risk.
  • BRCA status may also influence the type of chemotherapy prescribed.
  • It can help identify candidates for targeted therapies like PARP inhibitors.

Furthermore, BRCA testing helps family members understand their own risk and take preventive measures if necessary.

Biological Features of BRCA-Linked Breast Cancers

Research has shown that BRCA-related breast cancers often appear at a younger age. About 12% of breast cancer cases in women under 40 are linked to BRCA mutations. These cancers also have distinct characteristics:

  • BRCA1-related cancers are typically triple-negative (hormone receptor-negative and HER2-negative).
  • BRCA2-related cancers tend to be hormone receptor-positive and HER2-negative.

These distinctions matter because they influence treatment protocols and outcomes.

Expanded Genetic Panels

While BRCA testing is a cornerstone of cancer genetic testing, it is just one part of a broader diagnostic landscape. Comprehensive genetic panels may evaluate additional genes associated with hereditary cancer syndromes, offering a more complete risk profile.

Your genetic counselor can help determine whether broader testing is appropriate based on your family history and medical background.

Emotional and Ethical Considerations

Undergoing cancer genetic testing can be emotionally challenging. Concerns about future health, insurance implications, and how results affect family members are common. Genetic counselors play a crucial role in offering support and ensuring you understand the ethical and practical aspects of testing.

Questions to Ask Your Healthcare Team

If you’re considering BRCA testing, here are some key questions to discuss with your doctor or genetic counselor:

  • Should other members of my family be tested?
  • If I’m BRCA-positive, does that mean my children also carry the mutation?
  • How do the results impact my current or future cancer screening?
  • Are there preventive steps I can take to reduce my risk?
  • Could broader genetic testing uncover other risk factors?

Conclusion

BRCA testing is a powerful form of cancer genetic testing that can provide life-saving insights.

By identifying mutations in the BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes, individuals can take proactive steps to manage their health and reduce the risk of cancer.

Whether you’re seeking peace of mind or making treatment decisions, understanding your genetic makeup can be a vital tool in the fight against cancer.

If you suspect you may be at risk due to personal or family history, speak with your healthcare provider about BRCA testing.

Early knowledge truly empowers prevention and protection—not just for you, but for generations to come.

Discovering your genetic predisposition to cancer can be a powerful tool in your healthcare journey.

FAQs

What is BRCA genetic testing?

BRCA genetic testing is a type of cancer genetic testing that checks for mutations in the BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes. These genes help repair damaged DNA, and mutations can significantly increase the risk of breast, ovarian, prostate, and pancreatic cancers.

What does the term “Cancer Genetic Testing” mean?

Cancer genetic testing refers to the analysis of DNA to identify mutations that may lead to an increased risk of developing certain cancers. BRCA testing is one of the most well-known types, but there are several other gene panels used to assess cancer risk.

Who should consider BRCA or other cancer genetic testing?

You may consider BRCA testing or broader cancer genetic testing if you have:

  • A personal or family history of breast, ovarian, or other related cancers.
  • A relative with a known BRCA gene mutation.
  • Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry.
  • Early-onset breast cancer or multiple family members diagnosed with cancer.

How is BRCA testing performed?

BRCA testing is typically done using a blood or saliva sample. A healthcare provider collects the sample and sends it to a lab to check for mutations in the BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes.

How long does it take to get BRCA test results?

Most BRCA test results are available within two to four weeks. However, in urgent cases (like those affecting treatment plans), results may be expedited.

What do positive BRCA test results mean?

A positive result indicates that you carry a BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutation, which significantly raises your risk of breast and other cancers. However, it does not guarantee that you will develop cancer—it simply identifies a higher risk.

What are the options if I test positive for a BRCA mutation?

If you test positive, your healthcare provider may recommend:

  • More frequent cancer screenings.
  • Preventive medications.
  • Prophylactic (preventive) surgeries like mastectomy or oophorectomy.
  • Genetic testing for close family members.

Does a negative BRCA test mean I’m not at risk for cancer?

Not necessarily. A negative BRCA result means no BRCA mutation was found, but you may still be at risk due to other genetic or environmental factors. Broader cancer genetic testing might be advised to look for other mutations.

What does it mean to get a “variant of uncertain significance” (VUS)?

A VUS means that a genetic change was detected, but its impact on cancer risk is unknown. More research is needed to understand whether the variant is harmful or benign. Your genetic counselor will guide you on next steps.

What cancers are linked to BRCA mutations?

Besides breast cancer, BRCA mutations can increase your risk of:

  • Ovarian cancer
  • Prostate cancer
  • Pancreatic cancer
  • Fallopian tube and peritoneal cancers
  • Male breast cancer
  • Fanconi anemia (in children with two BRCA mutations)
