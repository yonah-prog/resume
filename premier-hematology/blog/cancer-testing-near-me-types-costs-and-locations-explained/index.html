Types of Cancer Testing Available

When you search for cancer testing near you, it’s helpful to understand the categories of cancer that testing can detect.

Different cancers require different methods of diagnosis—ranging from imaging to blood tests to biopsies.

Here’s a breakdown of the major categories of cancer and how they’re typically identified:

1. Solid Tumors

These cancers develop in specific organs or tissues and form solid masses.

Examples include:

  • Breast, lung, colon, prostate, kidney, liver, stomach, pancreatic, and brain tumors.

How they’re tested:

  • Imaging techniques such as MRI, CT scans, mammograms, and ultrasounds are often used.
  • Blood tests and biopsies may follow to confirm diagnosis and determine the type or stage.

2. Hematologic (Blood) Cancers

These affect the blood, bone marrow, and lymphatic system.

Examples include:

  • Leukemia, lymphoma (Hodgkin’s and non-Hodgkin’s), and multiple myeloma.

How they’re tested:

  • Blood tests are the primary tool for detection, including complete blood count (CBC), flow cytometry, and bone marrow biopsies.

3. Hormone-Related Cancers

These develop in organs influenced by hormones and often involve receptor testing.

Examples include:

  • Breast, prostate, endometrial (uterine), and thyroid cancers.

How they’re tested:

  • Blood tests to check hormone levels, imaging, and tissue biopsies.
  • For breast and prostate cancers, specific markers (like HER2 or PSA) are also evaluated.

4. Skin and Soft Tissue Cancers

These involve the skin, muscles, fat, nerves, and connective tissue.

Examples include:

  • Melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and sarcomas.

How they’re tested:

  • Dermatological exams, skin biopsies, and in some cases, imaging for deeper or more advanced tumors.

5. Gastrointestinal Cancers

These affect the digestive system from the esophagus to the rectum.

Examples include:

  • Esophageal, stomach (gastric), colon, rectal, liver, pancreatic, bile duct, and appendix cancers.

How they’re tested:

  • Endoscopy, colonoscopy, imaging, blood tests (like liver function tests), and biopsy of suspicious lesions.

6. Gynecological and Reproductive Cancers

Cancers that affect the female and male reproductive systems.

Examples include:

  • Ovarian, cervical, uterine (endometrial), vaginal, vulvar, testicular, and penile cancers.

How they’re tested:

  • Pap smears, pelvic exams, ultrasounds, tumor marker blood tests, and biopsies.

7. Neuroendocrine and Brain Cancers

These cancers originate in hormone-producing cells or in the brain and spinal cord.

Examples include:

  • Glioblastoma, astrocytoma, pituitary tumors, meningioma, and neuroendocrine tumors.

How they’re tested:

  • MRI and CT scans are primary tools for diagnosis.
  • Specialized blood or hormone tests and biopsies may follow for confirmation.

8. Rare and Genetic-Linked Cancers

These include inherited or uncommon cancer types, often diagnosed through genetic and molecular screening.

Examples include:

  • Neurofibromatosis-related tumors, gastrointestinal stromal tumors (GIST), and some triple-negative or HER2-positive breast cancers.

How they’re tested:

  • Genetic testing panels, blood biomarker analysis, and tissue sampling under molecular diagnostics.

Each category requires a tailored approach for testing and diagnosis.

That’s why it’s critical to consult a specialist who can guide you toward the right screening strategy based on your personal risk factors, symptoms, and family history.

What’s Included in Cancer Testing?

The term cancer testing covers a wide range of evaluations and technologies. Depending on your symptoms, family history, or health goals, you may undergo:

  • Imaging tests (MRI, CT scan, mammogram, PET scan)
  • Blood tests (tumor markers, complete blood counts)
  • Genetic testing (for hereditary cancer syndromes)
  • Biopsy and pathology
  • Molecular diagnostics
  • Urine or stool sample tests

Each type of cancer requires specific testing methods.

For example, breast cancer often starts with a mammogram and may lead to ultrasound or biopsy.

Blood cancers like leukemia are diagnosed with blood tests and bone marrow biopsies.

As always, consult with your provider to determine which tests are right for you.

Cancer Testing Costs in New York (What to Expect)

While we can’t provide exact prices, most cancer testing costs vary based on:

  • Type of test performed
  • Whether imaging is required
  • Need for follow-up biopsies
  • Whether genetic counseling is included
  • Insurance coverage
  • Location and type of healthcare provider

The good news is many cancer screenings are covered by insurance, especially when deemed medically necessary.

Preventive screenings (such as mammograms or colonoscopies) may be free under your health plan. Always confirm coverage with your insurance provider.

When Should You Get Tested?

You should consider cancer testing near you if:

  • You have a family history of cancer
  • You experience unexplained weight loss, fatigue, or pain
  • You notice lumps, swelling, or unusual bleeding
  • You’re over the age of 40 and due for routine screening
  • Your physician recommends genetic testing
  • You belong to a high-risk group based on ethnicity, lifestyle, or genetics

Early detection remains the key to successful treatment.

What Happens After Cancer Testing?

Once your results are in, your healthcare provider will discuss the findings. Depending on the outcome, the next steps may include:

  • Further testing
  • Specialist referral
  • Treatment planning
  • Lifestyle changes
  • Monitoring and follow-up screenings

Patients who test positive for cancer receive immediate support, often including referrals to oncologists, access to infusion therapy, or clinical trials.
