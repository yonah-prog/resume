Feeling Tired All the Time? It Might Not Be Aging — It Might Be Iron Deficiency

Premier Hematology & Oncology
2025 · 5 min read

It’s easy to dismiss tiredness as just part of getting older. Maybe you’ve started needing a nap every afternoon. Maybe a short walk leaves you winded, or you’ve noticed it’s harder to focus, harder to think clearly. It’s natural to assume: “I guess this is just what aging feels like.”

But what if it’s not?

For many older adults, the real culprit behind these symptoms isn’t age — it’s iron deficiency anemia. And it’s treatable.

What Is Iron Deficiency Anemia?

Iron deficiency anemia occurs when your body doesn’t have enough iron to produce healthy red blood cells. Without enough iron, your body struggles to carry oxygen efficiently — leaving you feeling tired, weak, and foggy.

It’s incredibly common in older adults. In fact, studies estimate that over 10% of people over 65 have anemia — and many don’t even know it.

Common Symptoms That Are Often Overlooked

Here are some signs of iron deficiency that are commonly mistaken for “normal” aging:

  • Fatigue – Even after a full night’s sleep
  • Shortness of breath – With light activity or climbing stairs
  • Brain fog – Trouble concentrating or remembering things
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Cold hands and feet
  • Pale skin or brittle nails
  • Chest discomfort or irregular heartbeat

These symptoms might seem subtle at first. But left untreated, iron deficiency can become serious — even leading to heart problems or mobility loss.

Why Older Adults Are at Risk

There are several reasons why iron deficiency is more common as we age:

  • Dietary changes (less iron intake)
  • Chronic conditions like kidney disease, gastrointestinal issues, or cancer
  • Use of certain medications (like blood thinners or antacids)
  • Slow internal bleeding, often from the GI tract, that goes unnoticed

That’s why it’s so important to take these symptoms seriously — and not write them off.

A Simple Test Could Change Everything

The good news is that a simple blood test can check your iron and ferritin levels. If a deficiency is found, your doctor may recommend dietary changes, supplements — or in more advanced cases, iron infusions to quickly and safely restore your body’s iron stores.
