When to See a Hematologist: Signs You Shouldn't Ignore

Premier Hematology & Oncology
2025 · 5 min read

Hematology is a specialized branch of medicine focusing on diseases related to blood, the bone marrow, and the lymphatic system.

A hematologist diagnoses and treats conditions such as anemia, blood clotting disorders, leukemia, and other blood-related illnesses.

Many symptoms of blood disorders may seem minor at first but can develop into serious conditions if left untreated.

The challenge is that these symptoms often overlap with other health issues, making it crucial to consult a specialist when persistent signs appear.

Searching for “hematology near me” in New York can help you connect with experienced specialists who can diagnose and treat blood disorders effectively.

This article delves deeper into the symptoms that require a visit to a hematologist, explaining the significance of each and why early intervention is crucial.

1. Persistent Fatigue and Weakness: Could It Be Anemia?

Fatigue is a common complaint among adults, often dismissed as a result of a busy lifestyle.

However, if you constantly feel weak, lightheaded, or short of breath despite proper rest, you may be experiencing a blood disorder such as anemia.

Why It Happens:

Anemia occurs when your blood lacks enough healthy red blood cells to carry sufficient oxygen to your body’s tissues.

The most common causes of anemia include:

  • Iron Deficiency Anemia – Caused by low iron levels, often due to poor diet, heavy periods, or chronic bleeding (such as from ulcers).
  • Vitamin B12 Deficiency Anemia – Occurs when the body does not absorb enough B12, leading to neurological symptoms and extreme fatigue.
  • Chronic Disease Anemia – Linked to conditions such as kidney disease, cancer, and rheumatoid arthritis.

When to See a Hematologist:

If fatigue is accompanied by dizziness, paleness, or cold extremities, it’s important to consult a hematologist for blood tests to diagnose anemia and start appropriate treatment.

2. Unexplained Bruising and Bleeding: Are Your Blood Platelets Low?

Most people bruise after an injury, but if you notice frequent bruises appearing without reason, or experience prolonged bleeding from minor cuts, you may have an underlying blood disorder.

Why It Happens:

Platelets are responsible for clotting blood and preventing excessive bleeding. A low platelet count (thrombocytopenia) can result from conditions such as:

  • Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP) – A disorder where the immune system attacks platelets.
  • Hemophilia – A genetic condition where blood doesn’t clot properly.
  • Von Willebrand Disease – A clotting disorder that can lead to excessive bleeding.

When to See a Hematologist:

If you experience frequent nosebleeds, gum bleeding, heavy periods, or unexplained bruising, a hematologist can run tests to check for platelet disorders and recommend treatment.

3. Recurring Infections: Is Your Immune System Compromised?

A strong immune system is crucial for fighting infections, but repeated or severe infections could indicate a problem with your white blood cells.

Why It Happens:

White blood cells help defend the body against bacteria and viruses.

A low white blood cell count may be caused by:

  • Leukemia – A type of blood cancer affecting white blood cell production.
  • Aplastic Anemia – A condition where the bone marrow fails to produce enough blood cells.
  • Autoimmune Disorders – Conditions like lupus can impact white blood cell function.

When to See a Hematologist:

If you suffer from frequent colds, pneumonia, or infections that take longer to heal, you should consult a hematologist for a blood count evaluation.

4. Swollen Lymph Nodes: A Possible Sign of Blood Cancer?

Lymph nodes are part of the immune system, filtering harmful substances.

They may swell due to infections, but persistent or painless swelling can be a warning sign.

Why It Happens:

Swollen lymph nodes can be linked to:

  • Lymphoma – A type of blood cancer affecting the lymphatic system.
  • Leukemia – Can cause lymph nodes to enlarge as cancerous cells spread.
  • Infections – Persistent infections can also cause prolonged swelling.

When to See a Hematologist:

If swollen lymph nodes last longer than two weeks and are not painful, or if they grow rapidly, a hematologist can perform tests to rule out serious conditions.

5. Shortness of Breath and Chest Pain: Could It Be a Blood Clot?

Breathing difficulties and chest pain can be symptoms of a variety of conditions, including anemia or clotting disorders.

Why It Happens:

  • Pulmonary Embolism (PE) – A blood clot that travels to the lungs, causing sudden breathlessness.
  • Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) – Clots in the legs can break off and move to the lungs, leading to life-threatening complications.
  • Severe Anemia – Can reduce oxygen supply, causing shortness of breath.

When to See a Hematologist:

If you experience sudden chest pain, difficulty breathing, or leg swelling, seek immediate medical attention.

A hematologist can evaluate your risk of clotting disorders.

6. Frequent Blood Clots: Are You at Risk for a Stroke?

Blood clotting is crucial for healing, but excessive or abnormal clot formation can lead to deep vein thrombosis (DVT), pulmonary embolism (PE), strokes, or heart attacks.

These conditions can become life-threatening if untreated.

Why It Happens:

  • Thrombophilia – A genetic disorder that increases clotting risk, particularly after surgery, prolonged immobility, or pregnancy.
  • Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APS) – An autoimmune condition that triggers excessive clot formation, leading to a higher risk of strokes, miscarriages, and heart disease.
  • Certain Cancers – Some cancers, including pancreatic, lung, and ovarian cancer, elevate clot risk. Cancer treatments, like chemotherapy, may also contribute.

When to See a Hematologist:

If you’ve had multiple unexplained clots, a hematologist can perform genetic tests and clotting assessments to identify any underlying disorders.

Early intervention with blood thinners, lifestyle changes, and preventive care can reduce risks.

7. Pale or Yellow Skin: A Sign of Blood Cell Destruction

Unusual skin color changes, such as paleness or jaundice (yellowing skin and eyes), may indicate anemia or excessive red blood cell destruction (hemolysis).

Why It Happens:

  • Hemolytic Anemia – A condition where red blood cells break down too quickly, leading to fatigue, shortness of breath, jaundice, and dark urine. Causes include autoimmune diseases, infections, and genetic disorders.
  • Liver Disorders – The liver helps process bilirubin, a substance released when red blood cells break down. If the liver isn’t functioning properly, bilirubin builds up, causing jaundice.

When to See a Hematologist:

If you experience persistent pallor, jaundice, or extreme fatigue, a hematologist can determine whether you have anemia, liver disease, or another blood-related condition.

8. Unexplained Weight Loss and Night Sweats: A Possible Cancer Indicator

If you’re losing weight without dietary changes or experiencing night sweats, it could indicate a serious blood disorder.

Unexplained weight loss of more than 10% of body weight in six months warrants a medical evaluation.

Why It Happens:

  • Lymphoma or Leukemia – Blood cancers that disrupt normal blood cell production, causing rapid weight loss, fever, sweating, and fatigue.
  • Chronic Infections – Conditions like tuberculosis, HIV, or autoimmune diseases can affect metabolism, leading to weight loss and immune dysfunction.

When to See a Hematologist:

If you experience significant weight loss, night sweats, fever, and fatigue for over a month, seek medical attention.

A hematologist may perform blood tests, bone marrow biopsies, and imaging scans to detect underlying diseases.

9. Tingling or Numbness in Hands and Feet: Poor Blood Circulation?

Tingling or numbness in the hands and feet can indicate poor blood circulation due to abnormal red blood cell production or blood flow disorders.

Why It Happens:

  • Polycythemia Vera – A rare disorder in which the bone marrow produces too many red blood cells, leading to thicker blood, reduced circulation, and increased clotting risk.
  • Sickle Cell Disease – A genetic disorder where red blood cells become abnormally shaped, restricting blood flow, causing pain, numbness, and potential organ damage.

When to See a Hematologist:

If you have persistent numbness, tingling, or cold hands and feet, a hematologist can check for circulatory issues or blood diseases.

Early detection helps prevent clots, nerve damage, and organ complications.

10. Family History of Blood Disorders: Should You Get Tested?

Many blood disorders are inherited, meaning a family history increases your risk. Sickle cell anemia, hemophilia, and clotting disorders are some conditions with strong genetic links.

Why It Happens:

  • Sickle Cell Disease – A genetic disorder that causes red blood cells to become crescent-shaped, leading to anemia, chronic pain, and organ damage.
  • Hemophilia – A condition where blood does not clot properly, resulting in excessive bleeding and bruising.

When to See a Hematologist:

If you have a family history of blood disorders, genetic screening can help assess your risk level and preventive care options.

A hematologist can provide guidance on lifestyle changes, monitoring strategies, and treatment plans to manage potential risks.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

When should I see a hematologist?

You should see a hematologist if you experience frequent blood clots, unexplained fatigue, persistent bruising or bleeding, pale or yellow skin, numbness in your hands and feet, or significant weight loss. If you have a family history of blood disorders, consulting a specialist can help with early diagnosis and preventive care.

Can frequent blood clots lead to serious health problems?

Yes, frequent or unexplained blood clots can be a sign of an underlying clotting disorder, increasing the risk of deep vein thrombosis (DVT), pulmonary embolism (PE), strokes, or heart attacks. A hematologist can conduct blood tests and genetic screenings to determine the cause and recommend appropriate treatment, such as blood thinners or lifestyle changes.

What are the signs of a blood disorder causing anemia?

Common symptoms of anemia include extreme fatigue, shortness of breath, dizziness, pale skin, cold hands and feet, and frequent headaches. In some cases, jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes) may indicate excessive red blood cell destruction (hemolysis). If you experience these symptoms, a hematologist can diagnose and treat the underlying cause.

Are night sweats and weight loss symptoms of a serious condition?

Yes, unexplained weight loss, excessive night sweats, and persistent fever may indicate lymphoma, leukemia, or chronic infections. If these symptoms persist for more than a month, it is crucial to see a hematologist for further evaluation, including blood tests and imaging scans.

How do I know if I have a genetic blood disorder?

If you have a family history of blood disorders such as sickle cell disease, hemophilia, or thrombophilia, genetic screening can assess your risk. A hematologist can provide guidance on monitoring, preventive care, and treatment options to help you manage potential complications before they arise.
