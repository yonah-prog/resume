Julian's Story

It started around the game.

Founder & Managing Partner · Brunswick Sports Management

Photo
Julian Aiken
Founder & Managing Partner · Published 2024
"Access without intention is just a story to tell at a party. Access with intention is an education you can't buy."

"How did you get into sports?"

It's the question I get asked the most.

The truth is, sports has been in my orbit my whole life, I just made a conscious choice to learn from it instead of just enjoy it.

As a kid, my uncle played 7 years in the NBA. Most kids in that situation would've been starstruck. I was curious. I watched. I listened. I asked questions. Not about the highlights — about how the business worked, how players carried themselves, how the people around them operated.

Years later, my brother's best friend Kyrie Irving was traded to the Boston Celtics, while he was playing at Harvard, and suddenly I had a front-row seat to how a global icon navigates the game, the business, and the spotlight.

Most people in that environment sit back. They take pictures. They tell their friends.

I did the opposite.

I observed. I shook hands. I introduced myself. I learned as much as I could from every conversation, every room, every interaction — the same way I'd done as a kid around my uncle.

Because access without intention is just a story to tell at a party. Access with intention is an education you can't buy.

But knowing the game and knowing the business are two different things. So I went to work, literally. Nearly two years, completely for free, just to get inside the industry. No paycheck. No promises. Just a bet on myself that if I learned the business cold, the right door would eventually open. Meanwhile, holding a full-time role in technology — a lot of late nights.

In 2018, it did.

Allonzo Trier of the New York Knicks gave me my shot. One opportunity. One yes. That turned into 6 clients by the end of that year and has since grown into managing 30+ clients at Brunswick Sports Management, with many of those relationships now over a decade deep.

The landscape

Here's what I tell anyone trying to break into this space:

Sports isn't one industry. It's a dozen industries wearing the same jersey.

You can work for a team — there are dozens of corporate roles inside one franchise. You can work for an agency, same thing. You can specialize as a financial advisor. A CPA handling athlete tax strategy. A marketing agent landing endorsement deals. A PA-certified contract agent negotiating contracts. Or you can build in new media as a podcaster, content creator, or storyteller shaping the culture itself.

Each one is a completely different game. You can't play them all. You have to pick your position.

And the landscape today looks nothing like it did in the '90s, when my uncle was playing. The athlete is the media company now. The platform is the leverage. The brand is the business.

So if you're trying to find your way in?

Don't chase the title. Chase the value you can uniquely create. Be the person in the room who observes more than they talk. Learn the business before you ask the business to pay you. And bet on yourself long enough for someone else to bet on you too.

"Don't chase the title. Chase the value you can uniquely create."

This is my life's work

Being a business manager isn't just a job — it's my life.

Leaving the tech world for the sports industry was never just a career pivot for me. It was a calling. It was a return to exactly who I am.

I come from a lineage of full scholarship and professional athletes. My uncle Bobby McCann played five years in the NBA. My dad was a Big East Champion running track at UConn. My brothers, Bryce Aiken & Kyle Alexander earned full rides to play basketball at Harvard, Seton Hall, and HBCU Lincoln. I walked that exact same path as a full scholarship athlete and a three-year captain at Assumption University. We lived and breathed this culture our entire lives.

But I also saw the gaps. I stepped into this business with a deep personal mission: to help athletes build out actual enterprises that eclipse their playing careers. To help them become so much more than just a name on a jersey. This is my life's work.

We understand that doing things differently comes with bumps and bruises, but we are built for it. After a career in tech, I have seen how industries must change, adapt, and modernize over time. With social media, content creation, and equity partnerships becoming the true drivers of value in today's society, we aren't just participating in the business management space — we are innovating it.

Tech entrepreneur Julian Aiken, a former DII point guard, is also no stranger to professional sports. His uncle, Bob McCann, played in the NBA for five years, and many of his high school friends grew up to be professional athletes. Aiken is the founder and CEO of Brunswick Sports Management, a firm that helps athletes transition to business. Recently, alongside the NBA trifecta of Michael Carter-Williams (retired), Mo Harkless (retired), and Andre Drummond (active), Aiken founded his latest endeavor: Special Teams Real Estate, a property venture that converts vacant commercial buildings into residential ones.
